Black Lives Matter Lesson Plan

The Black Lives Matter movement has been both an influential and controversial movement in recent America. In this lesson plan, students will examine the movement and its major players while creating a brochure to share what they learn.

Learning Objectives

By the conclusion of this lesson, students will:

  • Explain the development of the Black Lives Matter movement
  • Create a brochure about Black Lives Matter

Length

50 - 60 minutes (or 1 - 2 class periods)

Curriculum Standards

CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RH.6-8.3

Identify key steps in a text's description of a process related to history/social studies (e.g., how a bill becomes law, how interest rates are raised or lowered).

CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RH.6-8.7

Integrate visual information (e.g., in charts, graphs, photographs, videos, or maps) with other information in print and digital texts.

Materials

  • Printed copies of the lesson, Black Lives Matter Movement: Development, Goals & Impact
  • Printed copies of the quiz/worksheet
  • Student access to a computer program allowing them to create a brochure

Instructions

  • Begin by asking students what they know or have heard about Black Lives Matter.
  • Allow for some time for students to briefly discuss what they do know.
  • Give each student a printed copy of the lesson, Black Lives Matter Movement: Development, Goals & Impact.
  • Read through the lesson together, stopping occasionally to discuss:
    • What is the Black Lives Matter movement?
    • Who was Trayvon Martin and what did he have to do with the movement?
    • How did the movement spread?
    • Who was Michael Brown?
    • What are the goals of Black Lives Matter? What do you think about these goals?
    • How do you feel about the methods of Black Lives Matter?
  • Be sure to read and discuss the Lesson Summary thoroughly. Answer any questions students might have and allow students to briefly discuss and share their ideas and opinions (without letting things get too heated as this could be considered to be a controversial topic).
  • Check for understanding by having each student complete the quiz/worksheet.

Activity

  • Put students into groups of 4 - 5.
  • Give each group access to a computer with a program that will allow them to create a brochure.
  • Explain the activity to students:
    • Each group is to create a brochure that explains Black Lives Matter to the everyday citizen.
    • Each brochure must include information on the development of the movement as well as the goals going forward.
    • Each brochure must also attempt to explain and dissolve negative connotations commonly associated with Black Lives Matter.
    • Creativity, originality, visual interest, and the inclusion of all necessary information should be considered.
  • Allow time for the groups to confer and create their brochures.
  • When complete, allow each group to print out their brochures (perhaps enough to share one with each of the other groups).
  • Conclude the activity by allowing each group to share their work with the class.

Lesson Extensions

To extend learning, consider the following activities:

  • Have students research and give oral or written reports on some of the names commonly associated with Black Lives Matter (ie: Trayvon Martin, Michael Brown, Alicia Garza, etc.).
  • Have students develop a Venn diagram that compare the Black Lives Matter movement with the Civil Rights Movement of the 1950s and 1960s.
  • Encourage students to write an opinion piece concerning one of the events associated with Black Lives Matter.
