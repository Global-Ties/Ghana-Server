Separating Mixtures Lesson Plan

In this useful lesson, your students will learn about separating mixtures. They will view a video lesson on mixture separation, take a related quiz, and take part in a hands-on activity to reinforce newly acquired concepts.

Learning Objectives

After studying this lesson on separating mixtures, your students will be able to:

  • Describe several different methods for separating mixtures
  • Explain how these methods differ from one another
  • Recap what the process of chromatography entails

Length

1 - 1.5 Hours

Materials

  • Technology on which to play video lesson Chromatography, Distillation and Filtration: Methods of Separating Mixtures
  • Copies of the lesson quiz, one for each student
  • Beakers (two)
  • Chromatography paper (Optional)
  • Coffee filter
  • Flask
  • Funnel
  • Gravel (small, medium, and large pieces)
  • Internet access
  • Iron filings
  • Large bowl and small bowls (enough of the latter for one per each item in your mixture)
  • Magnet
  • Mixture of various items (that aren't sharp) such as bolts, Lego pieces, marbles, nuts, and screws
  • Paper plates
  • Sand
  • Solvent (optional)
  • Strainer

Instructions

  • Let your students know they are going to be studying methods for separating mixtures.
  • Ask them if anyone is familiar with the topic, or has even performed an experiment to separate mixtures.
  • Start the video titled Chromatography, Distillation and Filtration: Methods of Separating Mixtures and pause for the first time at 2:13.
    • Compare and contrast the two types of matter in existence.
    • Create your own definition of a mixture.
    • Analyze what is true about the components of a mixture, and what is a common example of a mixture.
    • How would you differentiate between a homogenous and heterogeneous mixture, and what is an example of each type?
  • Next restart the video and pause for a second time at 4:18.
    • Evaluate what it means to manually separate a mixture.
    • How would you use magnetism to separate a mixture?
    • How would you separate sand and salt if they are both mixed together in water?
    • How is brewing coffee a form of filtration?
    • When it comes to removing salt from salt water, how do evaporation and distillation differ?
  • Now restart the video and pause for a third and final time at 7:16.
    • Imagine what happens to ink when you spill water on a document.
    • What happens when you put chromatography paper into a solvent?
    • How does the process known as capillary action work?
    • How is a retention factor calculated?
    • Why is the retention factor never more than one?
  • Finally, resume the video and play to the end.
  • Review the entire video lesson with your students.
  • Have your students take the lesson quiz to determine their grasp on this new material on separating mixtures.

Activity

  • Let your students know they will be viewing a demonstration on separating mixtures, as well working as your assistants.
  • Do a quick review of the various methods of separating mixtures they just learned.

Part I

Note to Teacher - in advance of class, place your mixture of items such as bolts, Lego pieces, and so forth in one large bowl. Make sure they are really mixed together well.

  • Tell them the first demonstration will be to separate mixtures by hand. Now allow your students to pick out the pieces and put them in their correct smaller bowls.

Part II

Note - In advance of class, place the iron filings in a beaker with sand, and mix them together.

  • Inform the students they now will separate iron filings from sand by magnetism. Next gently place the magnet in the beaker (don't crack the glass) and allow your students to gather around and watch as the filings are pulled from the sand by the magnet.

Part III

  • Now place your mixture of gravel on a paper plate. Place your strainer over another empty paper plate, and pour the mixture through the strainer. The small gravel will pass through, but the medium and large pieces will remain.

Part IV

  • Next place a sand and water mixture in a beaker. Place the funnel on top of the flask, and place the coffee filter on top of the funnel. Then slowly pour the mixture into the filter. Ask your students what is happening to the mixture (the water goes into the flask, while the sand stays in the filter).

Part V (Optional)

  • Using chromatography paper and a solvent, demonstrate how a colored drop moves along the paper, as is detailed in the video lesson.

Extension

  • Adsorption chromatography is a specific type of chromatography. Write a one-page paper detailing this methodology of separating mixtures.
