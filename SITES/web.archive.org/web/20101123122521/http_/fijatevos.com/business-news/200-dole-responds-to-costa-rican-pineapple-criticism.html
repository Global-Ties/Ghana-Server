Dole Responds to Costa Rican Pineapple Criticism
User Rating: / 0
PoorBest 
Written by Russ Martin   
Friday, 08 October 2010 00:02

Dole Europe released a response at www.Fruitnet.com to allegations by Consumers International and Guardian Films on intensive tropical fruit production in Costa Rica.  An overview of the response and link to full page follows.

In brief, Dole Europe stated:

  • Denied that they maintain a chemical dump in Costa Rica, the accident related in the report was at a company airport.
  • The Acon Group no longer supplies them, but not due to any environmental issues.
  • The information about worker's cases cited as a part of the DBPC lawsuit was incomplete and invites those interested to see their presentation http://www.dole.com/CompanyInformation/DBCPFacts/tabid/1260/Default.aspx

In addition, they addressed other issues cited in the article.

Dole will not use, any product banned by the US - EPA or by the EU.

Dole discontinued Paraquat use in 2008

  • In Costa Rica, Dole's environmental and good agricultural practices are certified according to GlobalGAP, ISO 14001 and, in three of our four company-owned farms, Rainforest Alliance. Today, Dole in Costa Rica has dedicated 150ha to organic pineapple production and also sources organic pineapples from an independent producer.
  • Dole protects soil and water. For detailed information regarding our practices related to pineapple production in Costa Rica, feel free to contact us. These  programs limit soil erosion and reduce the use of herbicides, fertilizers and other agro-chemicals and contribute to protect groundwater.
  • They also state that their worker's wages, working conditions and freedom to organize are in accordance with international standards, for example the SA 8000 standard. SA 8000 is an internationally recognized standard based on the ILO conventions, the UN's Universal Declaration of Human Rights and the Convention on Rights of the Child. It focuses on child labor, forced labor, health and safety, freedom of association and the right to collective bargaining, discrimination, disciplinary practices, working hours, compensation and the implementation of an efficient management system.
