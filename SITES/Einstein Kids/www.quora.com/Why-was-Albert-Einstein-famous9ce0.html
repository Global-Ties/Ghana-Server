I am a big fan of Albert Einstein and though I have several engineering degrees, the reason I am such a big fan is not why he was so famous. The wisdom of Albert Einstein goes way beyond his scientific achievements. Let’s start with why he is famous and then I’ll explain why I am such an admirer.

Here is a short review of why he was so famous. He was a German-born theoretical physicist who developed the general theory of relativity, affecting a revolution in physics. For this achievement, he is often regarded as the father of modern physics and the most influential physicist of the 20th century. While best known for his mass-energy formula E = mc2(often dubbed "the world's most famous equation"),[he received the 1921 Nobel Prize in Physics for his services to theoretical physics, and especially for his discovery of the law of the photoelectric effect. The latter was pivotal in establishing quantum theory within physics.

Very impressive indeed, but not why I am such a big fan. To see his true wisdom and value you must study the man. I group his wisdom and value into 6 interesting categories. I will use these 6 categories to highlight Einstein’s true wisdom:

His Personality

While having a strong will, Einstein was very humble and attributed his talent to his passionate curiosity and imagination. He felt both were more important than knowledge. His view of was that the true sign of intelligence was not knowledge but imagination and believed that the only source of knowledge was experience.

Views on Life

A focus on simplicity was one of his main tenets and though his skill in physics was renown, he believed everything that can be counted did not necessarily count and that everything that counts cannot necessarily be counted. He believed in being a giver… only a life lived for others was a life worthwhile. Einstein was often frustrated with the state of his times and felt that the world would require a substantially new manner of thinking to survive. He felt that the world was dangerous not because evil people but because of people who recognize the evil, yet do nothing about it. His characterization of the age was that world leadership had developed a perfection of means yet had a significant confusion of ends.

Views on Creativity

His thought was felt that imagination was more valuable than logic … logic could get you from A to B, but imagination could take you everywhere. He was an early believer in trying many new things and not fearing mistakes … felt that someone who never made a mistake never tried new things. His view was any intelligent fool could make things bigger and more complex, but the genius and courage was in heading in the opposite direction.

Problem Solving Thinking

Einstein believed that if he was given 20 hours to solve a problem, he would spend 18 hours on understanding the problem and 2 hours on the solution. That is one of my favorite lessons from this thinker. He didn’t think he was that smart, it’s just that he had the skill of persistence to stay with problems longer than most people. Maintained a strong belief on the necessity of eliminating biases to be able to look for what is and not for what they should be. Always stated that problems required a new level of consciousness beyond the one that created the problem … he was a big proponent of change.

Education Views

Einstein was not a proponent of the education systems of his day. He used to say that one’s education was what was left after one had forgotten what one learned had learned in school. He also felt that it was a miracle that any curiosity was able to survive formal education.

His Sense of Humor

He often stated that his secret to creativity was knowing how to hide his sources. The difference between stupidity and genius he felt was that genius had its limits ... and liked to say that only 2 things were infinite, the universe and human stupidity, and he wasn’t sure about the former. Finally, he was a very observant person and noted that as he got more and more famous he got, the more and more stupid he also became. That he said was a very common phenomenon.

So maybe you’ll agree with me about the wisdom of Albert Einstein, eh? What is most amazing to me is the fact that most of his wisdom is more applicable in today’s society than it was in his. He was always considered ahead of his time wasn’t he?

Einstein is known for and credited for many things, but he is most known for:

  1. His 1905 Theory of Special Relativity
  2. His 1915 Theory of General Relativity
  3. Providing what is considered the most famous equation in the history of man, E=mc2, which was essential for the development of nuclear power, among many, many other things
  4. Providing us with the explanation of how gravity is created and how it works in the universe, overturning Sir Isaac Newton’s theory which had stood as truth for 250 years
  5. His theory of the photoelectric effect, which won him a Nobel Prize in 1921.

With the items listed above, Einstein is either directly or indirectly responsible for nuclear power, lasers and all laser technology, cell phones, laptop computers, computerized screens, PET Scans (positron emission technology), electron microscopes (allowed us to see atoms), satellites, spacecraft guidance systems, and the research and development of quantum physics.

Originally Answered: What was Einstein famous for?

In 1905 Einstein published four papers, any one of which would have earned him a place in history as a great physicist. Annus Mirabilis papers - Wikipedia The papers were, in order of publication:

1.) A paper deriving the photo-electric effect formula.

2.) An explanation of Brownian motion.

3.) The Special Theory of Relativity

4.) A paper making a case for the famous formula [math]E = mc^2[/math].

The first of these papers extended an idea introduced by Max Plank, that light is emitted as “quanta” with the energy of a quantum proportional to the frequency of light. Plank, building on the work of Lord Rayleigh and Sir James Jeans, was able to derive the experimentally observed distribution of energy, versus frequency, for blackbody radiation. By using a model of radiation as a collection of harmonic oscillators in thermal equilibrium with the walls of an enclosure, and the equipartition of energy theorem of thermodynamics, Rayleigh and Jeans had produced a distribution formula which agreed with the experimental observations at low frequencies. However, at higher frequencies the prediction of the Rayleigh-Jeans theory gave disastrous results, predicting the energy per frequency interval approached infinity with an increase of frequency.

By assuming that the energy of each mode of oscillation of the radiation must be “quantized,” Plank was able to arrive at the correct blackbody spectrum. Physicists, including Plank, regarded this assumption as merely an artifice to get the correct distribution of energy. The idea was that somehow the walls of the enclosure were only able to exchange energy with the radiation in discrete amounts, but that the radiation in the enclosure consisted of a superposition of classical plane waves of all frequencies.

Einstein, extended the idea of quantization in the exchange of energy, to quantization of the actual radiation itself, in other words that the radiation in the enclosure consisted of particles, we now call photons. This assumption resurrected the theory of Isaac Newton, that light consists of a stream of particles. The explanation, given by Einstein, for the photo-electric effect used this idea of light as particles, whose energy depended of frequency, to explain the photo-electric effect and derive a formula for it. The success of Einstein’s explanation of the photo-electric effect lead to the use of quantum ideas by others to explain physical phenomena, in particular Niels Bohr’s explanation of the hydrogen spectrum by assuming that the electron in the hydrogen could only occupy certain orbits and that transition from one orbit to another was accomplished through emission or absorption of quanta of radiation. These early ideas of quantization ultimately lead to modern quantum mechanics and quantum field theory.

The second paper of 1905 was the use of a phenomenon discovered by the botanist, Robert Brown, in 1827, and subsequently named Brownian motion. Brown noticed, while using a microscope to study pollen grains in water, that tiny particles moved about in the water in a random way. Einstein used this observation and molecular theory to calculate the mean squared displacement of the particles and to get an estimate of the size of molecules, and thereby determine how many water molecules are in a mole of water, and consequently Avogadro's number. Brownian motion - Wikipedia

The third paper of 1905 was called “On the Electrodynamics of Moving Bodies” (English translation) and is now known as The Special Theory of Relativity. This paper resolved a conflict between the branches of physics known as mechanics and electrodynamics. Essentially Einstein realized that the principle of Galilean relativity, that mechanical experiments could not distinguish a state of uniform motion from a state of rest, could not be extended to include the laws of electromagnetism without modification of our concepts of space and time. Our intuitive understanding of space and time, that lengths of measuring rods and intervals of time between events are the same for uniformly moving observers as for those we consider to be at rest, are based on everyday experience. Those intuitive assumptions are built into Newtonian mechanics. Einstein developed in special relativity a new formulation of mechanics based on ideas of space and time which different from those we derive from everyday life. The new concepts of space and time, and their relationship to each other, allowed the extension of the Galilean principle of relativity to electromagnetism, and indeed to all physical phenomena. A few years after Einstein published the paper on special relativity, Hermann Minkowski realized that the equations of special relativity implied a unification of space and time in a four dimensional world we call spacetime. This allowed Einstein to develop a theory of gravitation as a curvature of spacetime. This theory is called general relativity. The first confirmation of general relativity, the bending of light in a strong gravitational field, was observed in 1919 during a total eclipse of the sun and made Einstein instantly a worldwide celebrity. Subsequent predictions using general relativity are such phenomena as black holes, worm holes in space, and gravitational waves. The latter were first verified in 2015, one hundred years after the publication of general relativity.

The fourth paper of 1905 unified the concepts of mass and energy. The equation, [math]E = mc^2[/math] is possibly the most famous equation in physics and is the explanation of the source of nuclear energy, including the energy generated in the sun, and is the basis of production of particles in devices such as the accelerator at CERN, which allows us to test other theories in physics, such as the standard model. For more on this famous equation see The Three Meanings Of E=mc^2, Einstein's Most Famous Equation

Besides the papers of 1905 and the general theory of relativity, Einstein’s research involve such accomplishments as the prediction of stimulated emission, which is the basis for lasers, the search for a unification of electromagnetic theory and gravitation (carried on today in the expanded form of a search for a unified theory of all of the fundamental interactions in physics), and the prediction of Bose-Einstein condensates. The later subject is an active area of research in present day physics.
