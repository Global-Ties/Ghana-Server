By Michael Keller

Saeed Jabbar knows what it feels like to be excluded.

Born in the tiny South American country of Guyana, he moved to New York City with his family when he was 10. But some kids in his new neighborhood in Queens, didn’t take too kindly to the newcomer.

“It was a tough neighborhood, man,” recalled Jabbar, now 26. “I got picked on and bullied a lot. Staying home and playing video games kept me from getting beat up.”



Jabbar understandably developed a love for the games that kept him safe, an affection that evolved into a desire to make them. That’s when Jabbar started to teach himself how to code.&nbsp;

But instead of plying his skills exclusively for personal gain, Jabbar tapped into the isolation he felt as a boy to feed a budding concept: Help members of groups that are underrepresented in the tech industry find good jobs. &nbsp;

In 2016, Jabbar the coder became a founder of a nonprofit that teaches people of color the most in-demand tech skills. He called it Inclusion.



A Fork In The Road

Jabbar argues that people from underserved communities, including immigrants and refugees, lack opportunities to access jobs and gain the skills needed to obtain and keep them.&nbsp;

Data shows the size of the imbalance: Minorities are disproportionately missing from tech occupations like programming and data analytics.



Jabbar recognizes that while educational institutions and the tech industry are making inroads to address the disparity, more can be done to give people from these groups the tools they need to succeed.&nbsp;

That’s Inclusion’s reason for being. Jabbar and his team have trained more than 100 students — most are immigrants — in the skills they need to become web developers, back-end coders and project managers. It also connects newly trained graduates to people and jobs in the industry. The program, which is free to students, is backed by private donors and corporate sponsors.

Jabbar caught the eye of Samsung last year. &nbsp;

“Our goal for our mobile computing portfolio is to serve the needs of entrepreneurs like Saeed,” said Funbi Ibe, marketing director of mobile computing at Samsung Electronics America. “We want to develop technology that makes people more mobile but yet more connected, more productive but also more entertained. The work that Saeed is doing is so important, and we are inspired by all of the lives he has impacted.”

Jabbar, in turn, has become a fan of Samsung and counts the Samsung Galaxy Tab S4 as an essential item in his travel bag. When Jabbar attended CES in Las Vegas in January, he relied on his Tab S4 to stay productive. The portability of the 2-and-1 makes the Tab S4 his go-to computing device when he travels to conferences.&nbsp;

He’s also fond of the S Pen, the detachable keyboard, the Super AMOLED display and how quickly the device charges. He was also astounded to find that he could code and do dev work through DeX, a Samsung platform that creates a desktop PC experience on mobile devices.

“I was surprised at how much I use it,” Jabbar said. “With the S Pen that lets me sketch things out and the other functionality, it really adds a whole different dimension to the creative process.”

Launching New Lives

The hard skills Inclusion’s students learn — think JavaScript, CSS and HTML — are just part of the organization’s offering. In classes that are held in Queens and Manhattan, students also pick up soft skills like interviewing and networking while getting advice from mentors. Jabbar sometimes teams up with companies that are interested in creating a more diverse technical workforce.



“These are companies that get it,” he said. “They realize this isn’t about being a savior for people of color; it’s about saving money on hiring and getting more ideas in the room to solve problems.”

Students come into the three-month academy with jobs as bartenders, janitors and other lower-paying roles. When they get out, they land technical positions with salaries that can start at $60,000, an economic advance that catapults them into the middle class. So far, 73 percent of graduates have secured employment in this kind of work.

Khadijah Okoh is one of Inclusion’s many success stories. She took the training in 2017 when she was 23.&nbsp;

The Queens resident had recently earned a bachelor’s degree in computer technology from the City University of New York. She wanted to pick up more programming skills but couldn’t spend more money on education. By the end of the Inclusion program, she gained proficiency in JavaScript and tools like the database program MongoDB.

&nbsp;The organization also exposed her to the specialized field of ad tech, which uses digital technology in the advertising space. She landed a job at a wedding advertisement network, where she works on analytics and customer relationship management. Now she’s thinking of going back to school to get an MBA so she can help launch startups.

“Inclusion boosted my confidence and set me in the right direction to use my skills,” Okoh said. “Tech isn’t just about mastering languages; it's also all about networking. Inclusion is an opportunity to build your community and network, and potentially meet somebody who could change your life.”



Planning To Grow

Jabbar these days is focusing on his goals for 2019. At the top of his list is building a more robust community of alumni, sponsors and partners around the organization. This will allow Inclusion to stay connected to people throughout their careers and help graduates launch their own tech companies. Then he hopes to start satellite Inclusion programs in other cities.

For those big ambitions, he’ll need to ramp up his already frenetic life. Fueled by the transformations of his students, he’s confident he’ll succeed.&nbsp;

“People ask why I started Inclusion instead of something else in marketing or dev,” Jabbar said. “It’s watching the transformation from where our students were to where they go. And it’s how this change ripples through their family. It’s insane how we can improve people’s lives — that’s the oxygen that keeps me going.”
